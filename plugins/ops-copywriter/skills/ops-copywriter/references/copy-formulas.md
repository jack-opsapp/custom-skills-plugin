# OPS Copy Formulas

Proven copywriting frameworks adapted to OPS brand voice. Choose the framework that fits the format and objective.

## Framework 1: PAS-D (Problem - Agitate - Solve - Do)

**Best for:** Landing pages, long-form web copy, email sequences, blog posts

The workhorse framework. Start with the pain the reader already feels, twist the knife just enough to create urgency, present OPS as the obvious answer, then tell them exactly what to do.

### Structure

**P - Problem:** State the problem the reader recognizes instantly. Use their words, not marketing words.

**A - Agitate:** Make the cost of inaction concrete. Not fear-mongering - just truth. What are they losing? Time? Money? Their best crew member?

**S - Solve:** Introduce OPS as the fix. Be specific about HOW it solves the problem, not just THAT it does. One feature, one benefit, one proof point.

**D - Do:** Clear single action. Not "learn more." A real next step.

### OPS Example

**P:** "You're spending 2 hours every morning coordinating through group texts."

**A:** "That's 10 hours a week. 500 hours a year. At your billable rate, that's real money bleeding out of your business before anyone picks up a tool."

**S:** "OPS puts every crew member's schedule, job details, and assignments in one place. They open it. They know where to go. No morning calls. No 'what's the address?' texts."

**D:** "Try it free. Your crew will be using it by lunch."

## Framework 2: Hook - Prove - Push

**Best for:** Ads, social media, short-form content, CTAs, push notifications

For when attention is scarce. Lead with something that stops the scroll, back it up immediately, then push to action.

### Structure

**Hook:** One line that makes the reader's thumb stop scrolling. Usually a bold claim, a question they can't ignore, or a specific number.

**Prove:** 1-2 sentences of evidence. A testimonial snippet, a specific result, or a "how" that makes the hook believable.

**Push:** The CTA. Direct. One action. Low friction.

### OPS Example

**Hook:** "His foreman was about to get fired. Then they tried OPS."

**Prove:** "Bobby's crew went from chaos to organized in one week. His foreman went from liability to lead. Real quote: 'He's happy as a dog with two tails.'"

**Push:** "See what OPS does. Free to try."

## Framework 3: Show Don't Tell

**Best for:** Feature descriptions, testimonials, case studies, comparison content

Instead of claiming OPS is better, demonstrate it through specifics. Let the reader arrive at the conclusion themselves.

### Structure

**Show the before:** Paint the specific, concrete reality of life without OPS (or with a competitor).

**Show the after:** Paint the specific, concrete reality of life with OPS.

**Let the gap speak:** The reader does the math. Don't say "that's why OPS is better." They already know.

### OPS Example

**Before:** "Monday morning. 6 texts asking for addresses. 3 calls about who's riding with who. One guy shows up at the wrong site. Half your crew doesn't know the scope changed Friday. By 9am you've done zero actual work."

**After:** "Monday morning. Everyone opened OPS on the way in. They see the job, the address, the scope, their crew. Zero calls. Zero texts. Work starts at 7."

## Framework 4: The Anti-Pitch

**Best for:** FAQ sections, comparison pages, email, anywhere skepticism is high

OPS's secret weapon. Be so honest it's disarming. Acknowledge what the reader is already thinking and address it head-on. This builds trust faster than any claim.

### Structure

**Acknowledge the objection:** Say the thing they're thinking but won't say.

**Agree with part of it:** Don't fight their skepticism. Validate it.

**Reframe with truth:** Offer the real picture with genuine honesty.

### OPS Examples

"Why should I switch from Jobber?"
"Honestly? If Jobber works for you and your crew uses it, don't switch. OPS is for crews who tried Jobber and found it too complicated, or who are still using group texts."

"How do I know you won't shut down?"
"Fair question. I built this because I needed it. I'm not going anywhere. Month-to-month pricing means no risk for you. Your data exports anytime."

## Framework 5: The Origin Drop

**Best for:** About pages, founder quotes, credibility sections, email signatures, social bios

Use the founder's story as proof. The "I did this, so I built this" narrative.

### Structure

**Credential:** The real-world experience that gives authority.

**Frustration:** The specific problem that led to building OPS.

**Action:** What was built and why it's different.

### OPS Example

"I scaled a deck and railing business from $0 to $1.6M in 4 years. Tried every software on the market. My crew wouldn't use any of them. So I built something they would."

## Headline Formulas

Proven headline structures for OPS. Fill in the brackets.

### The Direct Claim
- "JOB MANAGEMENT YOUR CREW WILL ACTUALLY USE"
- "[BENEFIT] YOUR [AUDIENCE] WILL ACTUALLY [ACTION]"

### The Contrast
- "BUILT FOR THE FIELD, NOT THE OFFICE"
- "[BUILT FOR X], NOT [Y]"

### The Challenge
- "IF YOU CAN'T FIGURE IT OUT IN 60 SECONDS, IT FAILED"
- "[IMPOSSIBLY SIMPLE BENCHMARK]. [CONSEQUENCE IF IT DOESN'T]."

### The Problem Label
- "YOU'RE EITHER DROWNING IN CHAOS OR PAYING FOR SOFTWARE NOBODY USES"
- "YOU'RE EITHER [BAD OPTION A] OR [BAD OPTION B]"

### The Quiet Flex
- "CREWS THAT SWITCHED AREN'T GOING BACK"
- "[SOCIAL PROOF STATEMENT THAT IMPLIES QUALITY]"

### The Working-Class Declaration
- "YOUR CREW DESERVES SOFTWARE THAT WORKS AS HARD AS YOU DO"
- "YOUR [AUDIENCE] DESERVES [PRODUCT] THAT [MIRRORS THEIR WORK ETHIC]"

## CTA Formulas

### Primary CTAs (High commitment)
- "Get OPS free"
- "Start for free"
- "Try it - your crew will thank you"

### Secondary CTAs (Low commitment)
- "Try the tutorial first"
- "See how it works"
- "Talk to the founder"

### Urgency CTAs (Time-sensitive)
- "Your crew is waiting"
- "Stop losing hours. Start now."

### Rules for OPS CTAs
- Never use exclamation points
- Always lowercase "free" (it's not a gimmick, it's confidence)
- Prefer 4-6 words
- The CTA should complete the thought the headline started
- "Learn more" is never acceptable - be specific about what happens next

## Social Proof Formulas

### The Result Quote
"[Specific result] - [Name], [Trade], [Location]"

Example: "Saving me 2 hours daily of coordination." - Jorge R., Painting Contractor, Kelowna

### The Transformation
"We went from [before state] to [after state]."

Example: "We went from our crew ignoring the app to being excited to use it."

### The One-Liner
Short. Decisive. Undeniable.

Example: "It's an absolute game changer."

### The Story
A mini-narrative with specific detail that makes it believable.

Example: "I was on the verge of firing my foreman. We adopted OPS, and he's happy as a dog with two tails."

## Email Subject Line Formulas

### The Honest Question
- "Is your crew still using group texts?"
- "What did coordination cost you this week?"

### The Specific Claim
- "2 hours a day. That's what Jorge saved."
- "One app replaced his whiteboard, spreadsheet, and group chat."

### The Peer Proof
- "Why Ryan switched from Jobber"
- "What Bobby's foreman said after week one"

### The Challenge
- "60 seconds. That's all it should take."
- "If your crew won't open it, it's the software's fault."

### Rules
- No ALL CAPS in subject lines (save that for headlines)
- No emojis in subject lines
- No clickbait - the email must deliver on the subject
- 5-9 words ideal
- Lowercase feels more personal ("your crew is waiting" not "Your Crew Is Waiting")
